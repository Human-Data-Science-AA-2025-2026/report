\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Every year, tens of thousands of young Italians leave the country
in search of better career prospects, higher wages, or simply a change of
scenery.
Yet the official statistics that are supposed to track this movement are,
by the admission of the institutions that produce them,
structurally incomplete.
This is not a measurement problem in the conventional sense it is, at its root, a \emph{human} problem:
the data are generated by a bureaucratic act that people can choose to perform
or to ignore.

The \textit{Anagrafe degli Italiani Residenti all'Estero} (AIRE) is the
official register of Italian citizens residing abroad for more than
twelve months~\cite{legge470_1988}.
Registration is a legal obligation, but the administrative consequences of
non-compliance are negligible, and the perceived benefits are often insufficient to motivate action,
particularly among young and mobile individuals who may not yet identify as
\emph{permanent} emigrants~\cite{migrantes2022}.
The result is a register that is, by construction, an optimistic lower bound
on the true stock and flow of Italians living abroad.

Measuring the true extent of this undercount is not merely an academic exercise.
Public policies related to schooling abroad, pension contributions,
double taxation agreements, and political representation in the Italian
Parliament are calibrated on AIRE-derived figures~\cite{istat_migrazioni2023}.
If those data are wrong, the policies built on them are also wrong and the
people who are most affected are precisely those who are hardest to count.

In this study, we operationalise the undercount through
\emph{mirror statistics}~\cite{abel_cohen2019}:
a methodological approach in which the immigration records of receiving
countries are used as an independent estimate of the emigration flows from
the origin country.
Specifically, we compare the number of Italian emigrants aged between 18 and 35 years old
reported by ISTAT (sourced from AIRE) with the number of Italian-born immigrants
in the same age group recorded in the major EU destination countries
and reported to Eurostat.
Any persistent positive difference between the two series constitutes
evidence of systematic underreporting on the Italian side.

We frame this investigation within the conceptual triangle of \emph{Human Data Science},
which places human factors at the centre of the data-science pipeline.
The triangle has three vertices:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Science}: the statistical and computational methods we apply;
    \item \textbf{Data}: the AIRE/ISTAT and Eurostat datasets and their
    respective limitations;
    \item \textbf{Humans}: the emigrants whose behaviour determines what
    is and is not recorded, and the policy-makers whose decisions depend
    on the quality of the record.
\end{itemize}
