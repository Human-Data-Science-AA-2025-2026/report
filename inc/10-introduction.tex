\section{Introduction}
Every year, thousands of Italians leave the country in search of better employment
conditions, higher wages, or broader professional opportunities. This phenomenon is
widely discussed in public debate, but the data underpinning
policy decisions remain surprisingly unreliable. The root of the problem is structural:
Italy counts its emigrants through ISTAT, via the \emph{Anagrafe degli Italiani Residenti
all'Estero} (AIRE), a national registry that citizens residing abroad are legally required
to join~\cite{legge470_1988}. However, compliance is low, because registration carries no
immediate benefit and its omission involves no real enforcement. The result is a
self-reported dataset with a systematic downward bias.

This study investigates the magnitude of that bias. Rather than estimating the true
emigration figure from scratch, we adopt a \emph{mirror statistics} approach: we
compare ISTAT published emigration flows, which are derived from AIRE registrations,
against immigration records published independently by destination countries.
If Italy reports $x$ emigrants to Germany in a given year, Germany should
report approximately $x$ new Italian residents.
Systematic divergences between the two sides of the same flow reveal the extent of the undercount~\cite{abel_cohen2019}.

The implications are not merely academic. Migration statistics inform labor market
policies, pension projections, and public investment decisions. Behind each
undercounted emigrant is an individual who has chosen consciously or not to
remain invisible to the Italian state: someone who did not register with AIRE because
the process felt unnecessary, burdensome, or simply unknown. This collective behavior,
aggregated across thousands of people, distorts the data that policymakers rely on.
If the emigrating population is systematically undercounted, downstream decisions
rest on flawed premises. Understanding where the data breaks is a prerequisite for fixing it.
