\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{The AIRE Registration System and Its Structural Limits}
The \emph{Anagrafe degli Italiani Residenti all'Estero} was established by Law 470/1988~\cite{legge470_1988}
to maintain a record of Italian citizens living abroad for more than twelve consecutive
months. Registration is a legal obligation: citizens must notify their local municipality
before departing, or alternatively contact the nearest Italian consulate within ninety
days of establishing residency abroad. Municipal records feed into ISTAT's official
emigration statistics, making AIRE the primary source for any quantitative study of
Italian emigration~\cite{istat_migrazioni2023}.

In principle, the system is comprehensive. In practice, it suffers from a well-documented
compliance gap. Registration requires initiative from the emigrant: it confers no
immediate administrative benefit in the destination country, and non-compliance carries
no meaningful sanction. The result is a classic self-reporting problem, the kind
described in the data quality literature as \emph{administrative undercount}: the
registry captures only those individuals who chose, or remembered, to interact with
a bureaucratic procedure that felt optional. Fondazione Migrantes, which publishes an
annual audit of Italian emigration figures, has consistently reported that AIRE totals
fall short of estimates derived from consular records and destination-country
sources~\cite{migrantes2022}.

\subsection{Mirror Statistics as a Measurement Tool}
When a single administrative source is known to be incomplete, a standard approach
in demographic accounting is to cross-validate it against an independent source
recording the same event from the opposite side.

The method has been applied at the European level by Eurostat, which explicitly
acknowledges that emigration flows are harder to measure than immigration flows:
departure is rarely registered with the same administrative urgency as arrival, because
the receiving country's institutions — residency permits, tax registration, school
enrollment — create natural incentives for immigrants to make themselves visible to
authorities, incentives that do not exist on the departure side~\cite{eurostat_migration_explained2023, ec_regulation_862_2007}.
This asymmetry is structural, and it is precisely what our analysis exploits.
